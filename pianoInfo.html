Piano Information

I've been studying piano starting July 7, 2001.  I've practiced quite regularly (i.e., everyday) since (except when there wasn't a piano/keyboard present -- even then I would think about it and perhaps practice in my head).

I started reading books and working through a video/book self taught course for 8 months.  I learned how to read the notes on the grand staff, chords, etc.  I wasn't proficient at it and my tone was really bad but at least I had a reasonable working knowledge of music. At this point, I figured the best way to proceed would be lessons 1 day  (1 hour each day) per week which I did for about 4 months (my instructor and I went through Alfred's Book I and II in order of pages -- didn't complete II).  I then took two months off of lessons and then restarted.  I've been taking lessons 1 day (1/2 hour) per week ever since.

I stopped going through the Alfred's Books in order but refer to them from time to time to pick up info on music theory and perhaps songs that relate.  I spend most of my time looking for songs that I want to play; I learn , from each song, skills that I feel will make me a better player.  On a weekly basis I assess and figure out what skill I need to work on and then look for music to match and/or make up exercises.  I also listen to my instructor for advice and what things he thinks I should work on.

I keep this repertoire list around for my own sanity and to help me show how far I've come especially when I find myself having a difficult time playing a new piece.  In my list below, the composer is given when known.  In some cases, the composer is not given; instead I indicate someone who has performed the song, or I give the reference for the (sheet) music that I used.  The names of each piece are given; if the work "Easy" appears, this is a music arrangement geared toward early piano players (I find that "Easy" is a relative term).  If "Easy" does not appear, the piece is the original arrangement-- and NOT "Easy".

There are numerous songs that I practice "when I feel in the mood" that I don't list here because I never really seriously learned them.  These are songs that were meant for me to practice my sight reading and I actually don't want to memorize them because then I could no longer use them to practice sight reading anymore.

Below I includ links to some of my more "listenable" pieces.  I recorded these on my electronic keyboard (fullsize GEM RP7) and then encoded them using mp3.  I tried to get them all in 1 or 2 "takes"; I did not obssess over them and repeat and splice, etc.  They are all played as is.  Please forgive any mistakes (cut me some slack as I'm just finishing my third year).  These mp3s are part of my mp3 album entitled (appropriately I might add) "Humbly Me".  Some pieces are not complete as I am still developing my concentration and endurance at the piano.  Above all, enjoy!

Review of the Yamaha GP-1 (from a junior pianist)

I'm writing this review for those seeking a grand piano but have little or no knowledge.  I encourage you to look for other resources on the net to get familiar with these beautiful instruments and shop around and have fun.  I sort of regret not really shopping around.  I think this is because I just didn't know what to look for or how to play. On my next piano purchase, you can bet I will be more equipped to shop and will play as many pianos as possible before buying.  Now that I know kind of what I'm doing, it's very interesting to experience the various tones that different pianos make.

You can look up specs for the GP1 and the Yamaha web site so I won't detail them here.

I bought this piano as my first real piano.  I have a nice (i.e., formal) living room so I was concerned about looks.  I didn't know much about piano brands but knew the Yamaha name.  I saw that Sarah McLachlan was playing a Yamaha in her Mirror Ball video so that's what I wanted.

First of all, the piano is very "bright" sounding.  In the beginning, I liked this characteristic a lot.  Now, I find it a little annoying.  I find myself using the una corda pedal a lot to get the mellow sound I really want.  I've played Kawaii pianos and love their mellowness over the Yamaha.  I had someone come and "voice" the piano and that has helped a lot but I think the piano has gotten back some of that brightness over time.

The finish is beautiful but is easily scratched when cleaning with a vacuum cleaner attachment.  I use a soft dust cloth from now on.

My home has central air and heat and the humidity stays near 50% all the time and so the piano holds the tune really well.  I've noticed that at night (on cold nights outside), the piano sounds more mellow.  Perhaps because it's colder, etc. and that affects the wood.  It is at these times I am very drawn to the piano to play.

The touch is nice but firm.  Playing pianissimo is a challenge (perhaps this will get better with experience).  At night when the piano is more mellow, it is such a pleasure to play.  I believe it has gotten better over time.  It is just over a year old now.

I've recently discovered a few different ways to play the piano (and get different sounds).  These have allowed me to experience a "newness" about the piano (e.g., discovering something I didn't know about the piano) and have made owning the piano more exciting (esp. after the initial excitement of a new piano wore off):
  • Play with lid down and music holder up (standard way to play)
  • Play with lid up and music holder up (makes a louder and fuller sound)
  • Play with lid up and music holder down (makes a louder and clearer sound (my favorite))
  • Play with lid up and music holder piece removed so you can see the hammers (also louder and more clean but not as nice a sound as with music holder piece on (my opinion of course)
Also, playing with the una chorda pedal down has created interesting effects of sound.  Moonlight Sonata (Op. 27, No. 2, 1st movement) sounds spectacular when played with the lid up, music holder down and una chorda pedal used.  Of course "spectacular" is relative given that I haven't played that long
